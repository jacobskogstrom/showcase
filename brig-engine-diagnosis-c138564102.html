Brig Eagle 780 Overnighter · Evinrude E-TEC G2 300 HP

Engine stall diagnosis — root cause found

Engine died twice at sea with zero fault display, restarted instantly on a key cycle. EvDiag6 diagnostic pull (2026-07-10) traced it to a battery/ground connection fault, reproduced live at the dock.

Status: Root cause confirmed — battery terminal / ground / connection fault. Not the kill-switch, not the ignition switch, not fuel. Engine internals (temps, knock, oil pressure) read clean across all three EMM pulls.

Why "no fault code shown" was the key clue

The EMM (engine computer) actively monitors oil, fuel pressure, temperature, knock, and water pressure, and throws a visible fault plus a forced "get-home" mode for any of those. A clean stall with zero display message that restarts instantly on a key cycle doesn't match a monitored fault — it matches something that briefly killed the EMM's own power, which it can't log because it wasn't running.

Control head report Fault P32 “Accessory power disconnected” logged at a recent power-on cycle — the helm lost its battery feed at the same time as the engine side. Same root cause, two symptoms.

Dockside live datalog Idling at the dock, battery voltage dropped from ~15V to 3.4V in under a second, charge current spiked to 51A recovering it, then settled over ~8 seconds. Reproduced sitting still — not just triggered by wave shock at sea.

Engine health across all three EMM pulls: max head temp 84.9°C, no knock detected, EMM never exceeded 67°C. This is a power-delivery problem, not an engine problem.

What to check, in order

  1. Battery terminals + main cable lugs, both ends — battery post and engine-end lug. Clean corrosion, retorque. This single fix explains every fault code and the voltage drop.
  2. Battery switch — wiggle-test under load (engine idling) while watching live voltage.
  3. Ground cables — engine block ground strap, helm ground.
  4. Load-test the battery — a healthy battery shouldn't dip to 3.4V under normal idle load.
  5. "Fused B" fuse holder on the engine fuse block — corrosion there directly explains code 161.
  6. Repro test: engine idling at the dock, live voltage on screen, wiggle each cable/connector by hand — a voltage dip when you touch a specific point is the confirmation, no need to be back at sea.
  7. Starter solenoid connector (code 79) — inspect, likely a downstream artifact rather than its own fault.

Secondary findings — worth a mention at service, not urgent

ESC shift-position faults (codes 201/202/204/206/207/209) all logged in an earlier engine-hour window, none since — shifting fine now. Critical Low Oil detected 9 times historically, separate timeframe from the stall — worth checking the oil tank pickup/level sensor. EMM hours run lower than engine hours, consistent with an EMM swap that happened before purchase. None of these are linked to the stalling.
